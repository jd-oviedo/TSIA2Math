#### **Part 1: Guided Notes**

##### Three Costumes, One Number

Here is something the TSIA2 will test you on constantly: the same number can wear three different outfits.

$$\frac{1}{2} \text{ and } 0.5 \text{ and } 50\%$$

Those are not three numbers. That is one number, written three ways. A half of a pizza, five tenths of a pizza, and fifty percent of a pizza all put the same amount of food on your plate.

This matters because test questions rarely hand you a tidy list of fractions. They hand you a mix: a fraction here, a percent there, a decimal in the middle. Your job is to see through the costume to the number underneath.

If that feels hard right now, that is normal. It is not a sign you are bad at math. It is a sign you have not yet picked a strategy. Let us pick one.

---

##### The One Strategy You Need

**Convert everything to decimals, then compare.**

That is it. That is the whole method. Decimals are the common language. Once every value is a decimal, comparing is easy, because decimals line up by place value.

Why decimals instead of fractions? Because comparing $\frac{5}{8}$ to $\frac{3}{5}$ means hunting for a common denominator, but comparing $0.625$ to $0.6$ means looking at the tenths place. Decimals are less work and fewer mistakes.

---

##### Conversion 1: Fraction to Decimal

**Divide the top by the bottom.** The fraction bar means "divide."

$\frac{3}{5}$ means $3 \div 5 = 0.6$

$\frac{5}{8}$ means $5 \div 8 = 0.625$

$\frac{2}{3}$ means $2 \div 3 = 0.666...$

Say it out loud as you work: "top divided by bottom." Students lose more points to reversing this than to almost anything else. $\frac{3}{5}$ is $3 \div 5 = 0.6$, not $5 \div 3 = 1.67$. Look at the numbers and ask, "Should three-fifths be smaller than one or bigger than one?" Three-fifths is part of a whole, so it must be less than $1$. If your answer is $1.67$, you divided backward.

---

##### Conversion 2: Percent to Decimal

**Divide by $100$**, which moves the decimal point two places to the left.

$62\% = 0.62$

$8\% = 0.08$

$140\% = 1.4$

The word "percent" literally means "per hundred." So $62\%$ is $\frac{62}{100}$, which is $0.62$.

Two traps live here, and they are the most common errors on this topic:

- **Do not just delete the percent sign.** $62\%$ is not $62$. It is $0.62$. A number written with a percent sign is already a fraction of a hundred.
- **Do not move the decimal too far.** $62\%$ is $0.62$, not $0.062$. Count carefully: start at $62.$, move left twice, land on $0.62$.

---

##### Conversion 3: Decimal to Percent

Going the other direction, **multiply by $100$**, which moves the decimal point two places to the right.

$0.6 = 60\%$

$0.375 = 37.5\%$

$0.05 = 5\%$

To get a fraction all the way to a percent, do both steps: divide top by bottom, then multiply by $100$.

$\frac{3}{5} = 3 \div 5 = 0.6 = 60\%$

Notice that $\frac{3}{5}$ is $60\%$, not $0.6\%$. Once you have $0.6$, you are only halfway there. You still owe the $\times 100$.

##### Example 1: Order from least to greatest

Order these: $\frac{1}{4}$, $0.3$, $20\%$

Step 1: Convert everything to decimals.
- $\frac{1}{4} = 1 \div 4 = 0.25$
- $0.3$ is already a decimal
- $20\% = 0.2$

Step 2: Line them up and compare.
- $0.2 < 0.25 < 0.3$

Step 3: Translate back to the original forms.
- $20\%$, $\frac{1}{4}$, $0.3$

---

##### Example 2: The percent trap

Order these: $0.6$, $62\%$, $\frac{5}{8}$

Step 1: Convert.
- $0.6$ stays $0.6$
- $62\% = 0.62$
- $\frac{5}{8} = 5 \div 8 = 0.625$

Step 2: Compare. This one is tight, so add zeros to give every number the same number of decimal places.
- $0.600 < 0.620 < 0.625$

Step 3: Translate back.
- $0.6$, $62\%$, $\frac{5}{8}$

That trick of padding with zeros is worth keeping. $0.6$ and $0.600$ are the same number, and the padded version makes the comparison obvious.

---

##### Example 3: Longer is not larger

Which is greater, $0.75$ or $0.719$?

It is tempting to say $0.719$, because $719$ is a bigger number than $75$. That reasoning treats the digits like whole numbers, and it is wrong.

Compare place by place, left to right:
- Tenths: $7$ and $7$. Tie, keep going.
- Hundredths: $5$ and $1$. Five beats one. Stop.

$0.75 > 0.719$

More digits does not mean more value. Pad them out and the tie-break is clear: $0.750$ versus $0.719$.

---

##### Example 4: A word problem

A jacket costs \$60. One store takes $\frac{1}{4}$ off. Another takes $22\%$ off. Which saves more?

Step 1: Convert the discounts to decimals.
- $\frac{1}{4} = 0.25$
- $22\% = 0.22$

Step 2: Since $0.25 > 0.22$, the first store's discount is bigger. Confirm with the actual money.
- First store: $0.25 \times 60 = 15$, so \$15 off
- Second store: $0.22 \times 60 = 13.20$, so \$13.20 off

Step 3: Answer the question that was asked.
- The $\frac{1}{4}$ discount saves \$1.80 more.

---

##### The Five Traps

Before you practice, know what is hunting you:

1. **Dropping the percent sign.** $62\%$ is $0.62$, never $62$.
2. **Over-shifting the decimal.** $62\%$ is $0.62$, never $0.062$.
3. **Reading the digits.** $\frac{3}{5}$ is $0.6$, never $0.35$.
4. **Dividing backward.** $\frac{3}{5}$ is $3 \div 5$, never $5 \div 3$.
5. **Answering the wrong direction.** If the question says least to greatest, do not hand back greatest to least.

Every one of those is a habit, and habits can be retrained. When you miss a problem below, name the trap you fell into. Naming it is how you stop repeating it.
