#### **Part 1: Guided Notes**

##### You Cannot Compute It, So Estimate It

$\sqrt{20}$ has no exact decimal. It runs on forever without repeating, which makes it irrational. You will never write it down completely, and the TSIA2 knows that. So it does not ask you to compute it. It asks you to **place** it.

That is a friendlier question than it looks. You do not need the answer. You need to know which two integers it lives between, and which one it lives closer to. That is a skill you can learn in one sitting.

If square roots make you tense, here is the reframe. A square root asks a question in reverse. $\sqrt{20}$ asks: **what number, times itself, gives 20?** That is all. You already know $4 \times 4 = 16$ and $5 \times 5 = 25$. So the answer is stuck between 4 and 5. You just estimated a square root, and you did not do anything harder than multiplication you already knew.

---

##### First, Own the Perfect Squares

Everything in this topic runs on these. If you do not know them cold, the method has nothing to stand on.

| $n$ | $n^{2}$ |
|---|---|
| $1$ | $1$ |
| $2$ | $4$ |
| $3$ | $9$ |
| $4$ | $16$ |
| $5$ | $25$ |
| $6$ | $36$ |
| $7$ | $49$ |
| $8$ | $64$ |
| $9$ | $81$ |
| $10$ | $100$ |
| $11$ | $121$ |
| $12$ | $144$ |

Read that list backward too. $\sqrt{64} = 8$. $\sqrt{121} = 11$. Both directions, until it is automatic.

---

##### The Bracket Method

Three steps. Every time. No exceptions.

**Step 1:** Find the perfect square just **below** your number.  
**Step 2:** Find the perfect square just **above** your number.  
**Step 3:** Take the square root of each. Your answer is trapped between them.

**Example 1:** Where does $\sqrt{20}$ fall?

- Below: $16$. Above: $25$.
- $16 < 20 < 25$
- $\sqrt{16} < \sqrt{20} < \sqrt{25}$
- $4 < \sqrt{20} < 5$

$\sqrt{20}$ is between 4 and 5. Done.

Notice what happened in the middle line. Taking the square root of all three parts keeps the inequality pointing the same way. That works because square roots preserve order: bigger number in, bigger root out.

---

##### Second, Which End Is It Nearer?

"Between 4 and 5" is often enough. When a question wants a decimal estimate, go one step further and ask which end your number is closer to.

**Example 2:** Estimate $\sqrt{30}$.

- Below: $25$. Above: $36$. So $5 < \sqrt{30} < 6$.
- Now measure the distances. From 30 down to 25 is $5$. From 30 up to 36 is $6$.
- $30$ is a little closer to $25$, so $\sqrt{30}$ sits a little below the middle.
- Estimate: about $5.5$, maybe a hair under.

The true value is $5.477$. Your estimate of $5.5$ is close enough for any TSIA2 question.

**Example 3:** Estimate $\sqrt{85}$.

- Below: $81$. Above: $100$. So $9 < \sqrt{85} < 10$.
- From 85 down to 81 is $4$. From 85 up to 100 is $15$.
- $85$ is *much* closer to $81$, so $\sqrt{85}$ sits close to 9, not near the middle.
- Estimate: about $9.2$.

The true value is $9.219$. Notice you never guessed "halfway." Halfway is a habit, not a method. Measure both distances and let them tell you where to land.

---

##### Comparing a Root to a Decimal

This is the form the test likes most.

**Example 4:** Which is larger, $\sqrt{40}$ or $6.5$?

Step 1: Bracket the root.
- $36 < 40 < 49$, so $6 < \sqrt{40} < 7$.

Step 2: Which end is nearer? From 40 down to 36 is $4$; from 40 up to 49 is $9$. Closer to 36, so $\sqrt{40}$ sits nearer 6 than 7. Estimate about $6.3$.

Step 3: Compare.
- $6.3 < 6.5$, so $6.5$ is larger.

The true value is $6.325$. Your estimate did not need to be perfect. It only needed to be good enough to decide.

There is a second way to settle this one, and it is worth knowing because it is exact. **Square both sides.** $6.5^{2} = 42.25$, and $42.25 > 40$, so $6.5 > \sqrt{40}$. No estimating at all. When a comparison is too close to call, squaring turns it into a fact.

---

##### The Traps

These are the specific wrong turns students take. Name them now so you recognize them later.

**Halving.** $\sqrt{36}$ is $6$, not $18$. A square root is not "divide by two." Test it once on a number you know and the habit dies: if $\sqrt{36}$ were $18$, then $18 \times 18$ would be $36$, but it is $324$. Nowhere close.

**Reading the radicand as the answer.** In $\sqrt{20}$, the $20$ is the number going *in*, not coming out. $\sqrt{20}$ is about $4.5$, nowhere near $20$. This one bites hardest when the radicand is small: $\sqrt{3.5}$ is about $1.87$, not $3.5$.

**Squaring instead of rooting.** $\sqrt{49}$ is $7$. $49^{2}$ is $2401$. The two operations undo each other; do not run them the same direction.

**Grabbing the wrong bracket.** For $\sqrt{55}$, the neighbors are $49$ and $64$, giving 7 and 8. Not $25$ and $36$. Slow down and find the perfect squares that actually surround your number.

**Defaulting to halfway.** $\sqrt{85}$ is $9.2$, not $9.5$. Measure both distances before you place it.

---

##### One Last Thing

Every square root of a non-perfect square is irrational. $\sqrt{20}$, $\sqrt{30}$, $\sqrt{85}$: none of them can be written as a fraction, and their decimals never end or repeat. But $\sqrt{49} = 7$ is rational, because 49 *is* a perfect square. The radical symbol does not decide. What is underneath it does.
