#### **Part 1: Guided Notes**

##### Why Bother Memorizing?

In QR.1.2 you learned the strategy: to compare values in different forms, turn everything into decimals. That strategy works every time. But it has a cost, and the cost is time.

The TSIA2 is not a race, but it is finite. If you stop and do long division every time you meet $\frac{3}{4}$, you will spend your test doing arithmetic you already know. Students who do well on this section are not faster at dividing. They just stopped dividing the common ones a long time ago, because they recognize them the way you recognize a friend's face.

This topic is about building that recognition. Not tricks. Just a small set of facts worth owning.

---

##### The Method Underneath

Before memorizing, understand the operation, because you will need it for fractions that are not on the list.

**A fraction is a division problem.** The bar means "divide." The top goes inside the box, the bottom goes outside.

$\frac{3}{4}$ means $3 \div 4 = 0.75$

The order matters and it is the single most common place students lose points. $\frac{3}{4}$ is $3 \div 4 = 0.75$. It is not $4 \div 3 = 1.333...$

Here is a check that costs you one second. **Is the fraction less than 1 or greater than 1?** If the top is smaller than the bottom, the answer must be less than 1. $\frac{3}{4}$ has a smaller top, so $0.75$ makes sense and $1.333$ does not. Run that check every single time.

---

##### The Halves, Fourths, and Eighths

Start here. These are the most common family on the test, and they follow a pattern worth seeing.

| Fraction | Decimal |
|---|---|
| $\frac{1}{2}$ | $0.5$ |
| $\frac{1}{4}$ | $0.25$ |
| $\frac{3}{4}$ | $0.75$ |
| $\frac{1}{8}$ | $0.125$ |
| $\frac{3}{8}$ | $0.375$ |
| $\frac{5}{8}$ | $0.625$ |
| $\frac{7}{8}$ | $0.875$ |

Look at the eighths. Each step up adds $0.125$. Once you know $\frac{1}{8} = 0.125$, you can build the rest by adding. And notice $\frac{2}{8}$ is missing, because $\frac{2}{8} = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25$. The list only holds fractions already in lowest terms.

---

##### The Fifths and Tenths

These are the friendliest, because fifths and tenths land on clean decimals.

| Fraction | Decimal |
|---|---|
| $\frac{1}{5}$ | $0.2$ |
| $\frac{2}{5}$ | $0.4$ |
| $\frac{3}{5}$ | $0.6$ |
| $\frac{4}{5}$ | $0.8$ |
| $\frac{1}{10}$ | $0.1$ |
| $\frac{3}{10}$ | $0.3$ |

The fifths follow a rule you can do in your head: **multiply the top by 2, put it in the tenths place.** $\frac{3}{5}$ becomes $3 \times 2 = 6$, so $0.6$. That works because $\frac{3}{5} = \frac{6}{10}$.

---

##### The Thirds and Sixths: Where Decimals Misbehave

Now the ones that do not end.

| Fraction | Decimal |
|---|---|
| $\frac{1}{3}$ | $0.333...$ |
| $\frac{2}{3}$ | $0.666...$ |
| $\frac{1}{6}$ | $0.1666...$ |
| $\frac{5}{6}$ | $0.8333...$ |

Divide $1$ by $3$ and the $3$s never stop. We write that as $0.\overline{3}$, where the bar means "this digit repeats forever."

Two things students get wrong here, and both matter.

**First: a repeating decimal is still rational.** It never ends, but it never stops following a pattern either, and it came from a fraction. $\frac{1}{3}$ is a fraction, so $0.333...$ is rational. Forever is not the same as random. (Compare $\sqrt{2} = 1.41421356...$, which never ends *and* never repeats. That one is irrational.)

**Second: do not chop it off and forget.** $\frac{2}{3}$ is $0.666...$, not $0.66$. Those are different numbers. $0.66$ is a bit smaller. If a question asks you to compare $\frac{2}{3}$ with $0.67$, chopping to $0.66$ gives you the wrong answer, because $0.666... < 0.67$ is true but $0.66$ makes the gap look bigger than it is. Round only at the end, and only if asked.

---

##### Which Fractions Terminate?

Here is a fact that feels like magic but is just arithmetic.

**A fraction in lowest terms terminates only if its denominator is built from 2s and 5s.**

- $8 = 2 \times 2 \times 2$, only 2s, so $\frac{1}{8}$ terminates
- $5$, only 5s, so $\frac{1}{5}$ terminates
- $10 = 2 \times 5$, so $\frac{1}{10}$ terminates
- $3$ has a 3 in it, so $\frac{1}{3}$ repeats
- $6 = 2 \times 3$, that 3 ruins it, so $\frac{1}{6}$ repeats

Why? Our number system is base ten, and $10 = 2 \times 5$. A denominator made only of 2s and 5s can be rewritten with a power of ten underneath. A 3 cannot.

You will not be asked to prove this. But if a problem asks "which of these repeats," you can answer by looking at the denominator instead of dividing four times.

##### Going Backward: Decimal to Fraction

Read the decimal out loud and write what you hear.

**Example 1:** Convert $0.25$ to a fraction.

Step 1: Say it. "Twenty-five hundredths."
- $0.25 = \frac{25}{100}$

Step 2: Simplify. Both parts divide by $25$.
- $\frac{25 \div 25}{100 \div 25} = \frac{1}{4}$

Step 3: Answer.
- $0.25 = \frac{1}{4}$

**Example 2:** Convert $0.375$ to a fraction.

Step 1: Say it. "Three hundred seventy-five thousandths." Three decimal places means thousandths.
- $0.375 = \frac{375}{1000}$

Step 2: Simplify. Both divide by $125$.
- $\frac{375 \div 125}{1000 \div 125} = \frac{3}{8}$

Step 3: Answer.
- $0.375 = \frac{3}{8}$

The place value tells you the denominator: one decimal place means tenths, two means hundredths, three means thousandths. Then reduce. **Do not stop before reducing.** $\frac{375}{1000}$ is correct but unfinished, and a test question asking for lowest terms will not accept it.

---

##### Example 3: Using What You Know

Which is larger, $\frac{7}{8}$ or $0.85$?

Step 1: Recall, do not divide. $\frac{7}{8} = 0.875$.

Step 2: Pad to the same number of places.
- $0.875$ versus $0.850$

Step 3: Compare place by place. Tenths tie at $8$. Hundredths: $7$ beats $5$.

$\frac{7}{8} > 0.85$

That took seconds because you did not do long division. That is the whole point of this topic.

---

##### Before You Practice

Four traps, all of them habits:

1. **Dividing backward.** $\frac{3}{4} = 3 \div 4$. Sanity check: small top means answer under 1.
2. **Reading the digits.** $\frac{3}{4}$ is $0.75$, never $0.34$.
3. **Chopping the repeat.** $\frac{2}{3}$ is $0.666...$, not $0.66$.
4. **Stopping before lowest terms.** $\frac{375}{1000}$ is not a finished answer.

If you miss one below, name the trap. That is how it stops owning you.
