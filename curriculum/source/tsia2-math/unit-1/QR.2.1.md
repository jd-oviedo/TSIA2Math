#### **Part 1: Guided Notes**

##### A Ratio Is a Recipe

You already use ratios. When you double a recipe, you do not add two of everything. You double everything. That instinct is the whole topic.

A **ratio** compares two quantities. We write it as $3:1$ or $\frac{3}{1}$, and we read it "three to one." If a recipe uses flour and sugar in a $3:1$ ratio, it means: for every 3 cups of flour, 1 cup of sugar. Not 3 cups total. Not 3 more cups of flour than sugar. **For every.**

That phrase is the key that unlocks every problem below. Whenever a ratio confuses you, say it out loud with "for every" in the middle.

---

##### The Mistake That Costs the Most Points

Here is the error the TSIA2 loves to test, because it is so tempting.

A recipe uses flour and sugar in a $3:1$ ratio. You use 12 cups of flour. How much sugar?

The trap: "Flour went from 3 up to 12. That is 9 more. So sugar goes from 1 up to 10."

That is wrong, and it is wrong in an interesting way. **Ratios scale by multiplying, not by adding.** You did not add 9 cups of flour to the recipe. You made the recipe **4 times bigger**, because $12 = 3 \times 4$. So everything gets 4 times bigger. Sugar becomes $1 \times 4 = 4$.

Test it against your kitchen sense. If a $3:1$ recipe became $12:10$, that is nearly equal parts flour and sugar. You would be eating a completely different dessert. The ratio has to stay $3:1$, and $12:4$ does, because $\frac{12}{4} = 3$.

**Ratios are multiplicative. Always.** If you catch yourself adding the difference, stop.

---

##### The Scale Factor Method

Three steps. It never fails.

**Step 1:** Find the **scale factor**. Divide the number you were given by its matching part in the ratio.  
**Step 2:** Multiply the *other* part of the ratio by that scale factor.  
**Step 3:** Answer the question that was actually asked.

**Example 1:** Boys to girls is $2:3$. There are 8 boys. How many girls?

- Step 1: Boys match the $2$. Scale factor is $8 \div 2 = 4$.
- Step 2: Girls match the $3$. So girls $= 3 \times 4 = 12$.
- Step 3: The question asked for girls. Answer: 12.

Check it. Is $8:12$ the same ratio as $2:3$? Divide both by 4 and you get $2:3$. Yes.

**Example 2:** A map uses $1$ inch $: 50$ miles. How many miles is 4 inches?

- Step 1: Inches match the $1$. Scale factor is $4 \div 1 = 4$.
- Step 2: Miles match the $50$. So miles $= 50 \times 4 = 200$.
- Step 3: Answer: 200 miles.

Step 3 sounds trivial. It is not. Many students find the scale factor of 4 and write down 4, because it was the last number they computed. The scale factor is a tool, not an answer.

---

##### Part to Part Versus Part to Whole

This distinction separates the Basic problems from the Proficient ones. Read carefully.

A ratio of green to yellow candies is $3:2$. That is **part to part**: it compares greens against yellows. It does *not* say greens are $\frac{3}{2}$ of the bag, which would be more than the whole bag and is impossible.

To get the whole, **add the parts**: $3 + 2 = 5$ total parts.

- Greens are $\frac{3}{5}$ of the bag.
- Yellows are $\frac{2}{5}$ of the bag.

**Example 3:** Green to yellow is $3:2$. There are 30 candies total. How many greens?

- Step 1: Add the parts. $3 + 2 = 5$ parts.
- Step 2: Find what one part is worth. $30 \div 5 = 6$ candies per part.
- Step 3: Greens are 3 parts. $3 \times 6 = 18$.

Check: greens 18, yellows $2 \times 6 = 12$, total $18 + 12 = 30$. And $18:12$ reduces to $3:2$. Both checks pass.

The trap here is dividing by the wrong number. $30 \div 3 = 10$ feels natural because 3 is right there in the problem, but 3 is not the total. **5** is the total. When a problem gives you a total, your first move is always to add the parts.

---

##### Rates Are Ratios in Disguise

**Example 4:** A store sells 3 notebooks for \$12. At the same rate, what do 7 notebooks cost?

You could use a scale factor, but $7 \div 3$ is not clean. So use the **unit rate** instead: find the cost of one, then scale.

- Step 1: Divide to find the cost of one notebook: $12 \div 3 = 4$. So one notebook is \$4.
- Step 2: Multiply up to seven: $7 \times 4 = 28$. So seven notebooks cost \$28.

Same idea, friendlier arithmetic. And note step 2 again: the answer is \$28, not \$4. The unit rate was a stepping stone.

---

##### Chaining Two Ratios

**Example 5:** $A$ to $B$ is $3:4$. $B$ to $C$ is $4:5$. If $A = 9$, what is $C$?

Walk it one link at a time.

- $A:B = 3:4$, and $A = 9$, so the scale factor is $9 \div 3 = 3$. Then $B = 4 \times 3 = 12$.
- $B:C = 4:5$, and $B = 12$, so the scale factor is $12 \div 4 = 3$. Then $C = 5 \times 3 = 15$.

$C = 15$. The middle value $B = 12$ was scaffolding, not the answer. Reread the question before you bubble.

---

##### The Four Traps

1. **Adding instead of scaling.** $3:1$ with 12 flour gives 4 sugar, not 10. Ratios multiply.
2. **Flipping the ratio.** $2:3$ with 8 boys gives 12 girls, not $5.3$. Match each number to its own part.
3. **Forgetting to add the parts.** With a total, $3:2$ means 5 parts. Divide by 5, not by 3.
4. **Answering the middle step.** The scale factor and the unit rate are tools. Read the question again before you answer.

Every one is a habit. Name the one you fall into below, and it loses its grip.
